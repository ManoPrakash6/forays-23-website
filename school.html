*Counting is fun*
Abstract
Abstract
Learning to count is the first step we all take into the world of mathematics. *Combinatorics* is the branch of mathematics that deals with counting objects of specified kinds. Over the last 60 years, it has been refined into a fine art, encompassing numerous beautiful ideas and techniques. In this talk, we will sample a few of them. For example, imagine you are moving on the number line starting at the origin and jumping one step left or right each time. How many different paths are there which return to the origin in 10 steps ? What if you are not allowed to go to the negative numbers ? Come let's explore.
Prof. Swati Sircar

A decade-long mathematical journey
Abstract
Abstract
This is a journey in four parts. When a state textbook for Class 6 changed a picture related to a geometry activity, we wondered how necessary that change was. The exploration led to a discovery with an interesting parallel to a known result. This in turn generated another question from a physicist friend and we went through a deeper second exploration. There were two rounds- an algebraic approch followed by an geometric one. While the formar got the results part by part, the latter got us deep insight at one stroke This second exploration also revealed some unforeseen scenarios. The third part was caused by a mistake that turned out to be invaluable and not only nailed the second exploration compleately but also dealt with the unforeseen scenarios. It demanded us to think in 3D and not in just 2D. The last part involved figuring out when each of the possible scenarios occur. It included finding the locus of a point generating the scenario that acted as a boundary between the other two.
Prof. Jonaki Ghosh
Lady Shriram College,
New Delhi

DEVELOPING MATHEMATICAL THINKING THROUGH PROBLEM SOLVING AND EXPLORATIONS
Abstract
Abstract
Learning mathematics is not merely about applying algorithms and procedures to solve textbook problems and perform calculations. It is about exploring ideas and phenomena using mathematics, analysing real life problems and developing mathematical thinking. The position paper on Teaching of Mathematics of the National Curriculum Framework 2005 strongly recommends in its vision statement, that children should be given the opportunity to ‘pose and solve meaningful problems’. Mathematics is the only subject at the school level, which formally addresses problem solving as a skill. However it is important to familiarise the student with a variety of problems which require abstraction, estimation, drawing analogies, reducing to simpler terms, guess and verify and generalization to arrive at the solution.

In this interactive session, participants will explore counting and colouring problems, paradoxes in probability and simulation of games.The emphasis will be on engaging with the processes of mathematical problem solving andelicit the true nature of mathematical thinking.
